Digital gardens let you cultivate your own little bit of the internet

A growing number of people are creating individualized, creative sites that eschew the one-size-fits-all look and feel of social media

By Tanya Basu

Sara Garner had a nagging feeling something wasn‚Äôt quite right.

A software engineer, she was revamping her personal site, but it just didn‚Äôt feel like her. Sure, it had the requisite links to her social media and her professional work, but it didn‚Äôt really reflect her personality. So she created a page focused on museums, which she is obsessed with. It‚Äôs still under construction, but she envisions a page that includes thoughts on her favorite museums, describes the emotions they evoked, and invites others to share their favorite museums and what they‚Äôve learned.

‚ÄúI‚Äôm going for a feeling of wonderment, a connection across time,‚Äù she says.

Welcome to the world of ‚Äúdigital gardens.‚Äù These creative reimaginings of blogs have quietly taken nerdier corners of the internet by storm. A growing movement of people are tooling with back-end code to create sites that are more collage-like and artsy, in the vein of Myspace and Tumblr‚Äîless predictable and formatted than Facebook and Twitter. Digital gardens explore a wide variety of topics and are frequently adjusted and changed to show growth and learning, particularly among people with niche interests. Through them, people are creating an internet that is less about connections and feedback, and more about quiet spaces they can call their own.

"Everyone does their own weird thing"

The movement might be gaining steam now, but its roots date back to 1998, when Mark Bernstein introduced the idea of the ‚Äúhypertext garden,‚Äù arguing for spaces on the internet that let a person wade into the unknown. ‚ÄúGardens ‚Ä¶ lie between farmland and wilderness,‚Äù he wrote. ‚ÄúThe garden is farmland that delights the senses, designed for delight rather than commodity.‚Äù (His digital garden includes a recent review of a Bay Area carbonara dish and reflections on his favorite essays.)

The new wave of digital gardens discuss books and movies, with introspective journal entries; others offer thoughts on philosophy and politics. Some are works of art in themselves, visual masterpieces that invite the viewer to explore; others are simpler and more utilitarian, using Google Docs or Wordpress templates to share intensely personal lists. Avid readers in particular have embraced the concept, sharing creative, beautiful digital bookshelves that illustrate their reading journey.

Beneath the umbrella term, however, digital gardens don‚Äôt follow rules. They‚Äôre not blogs, short for ‚Äúweblogs,‚Äù a term that suggests a time-stamped record of thought. They‚Äôre not a social-media platform‚Äîconnections are made, but often it‚Äôs through linking to other digital gardens, or gathering in forums like Reddit and Telegram to nerd out over code.

Tom Critchlow, a consultant who has been cultivating his digital garden for years, spells out the main difference between old-school blogging and digital gardening. ‚ÄúWith blogging, you‚Äôre talking to a large audience,‚Äù he says. ‚ÄúWith digital gardening, you‚Äôre talking to yourself. You focus on what you want to cultivate over time.‚Äù

What they have in common is that they can be edited at any time to reflect evolution and change. The idea is similar to editing a Wikipedia entry, though digital gardens are not meant to be the ultimate word on a topic. As a slower, clunkier way to explore the internet, they revel in not being the definitive source, just a source, says Mike Caulfield, a digital literacy expert at Washington State University.

In fact, the whole point of digital gardens is that they can grow and change, and that various pages on the same topic can coexist. ‚ÄúIt‚Äôs less about iterative learning and more about public learning,‚Äù says Maggie Appleton, a designer. Appleton‚Äôs digital garden, for example, includes thoughts on plant-based meat, book reviews, and digressions on Javascript and magical capitalism. It is ‚Äúan open collection of notes, resources, sketches, and explorations I‚Äôm currently cultivating,‚Äù its introduction declares. ‚ÄúSome notes are Seedlings, some are budding, and some are fully grown Evergreen[s].‚Äù

Caulfield, who has researched misinformation and disinformation, wrote a blog post in 2015 on the ‚Äútechnopastoral,‚Äù in which he described the federated wiki structure promoted by computer programmer Ward Cunningham, who thought the internet should support a ‚Äúchorus of voices‚Äù rather than the few rewarded on social media today.

‚ÄúThe stream has dominated our lives since the mid-2000s,‚Äù Caulfield says. But it means people are either posting content or consuming it. And, Caulfield says, the internet as it stands rewards shock value and dumbing things down. ‚ÄúBy engaging in digital gardening, you are constantly finding new connections, more depth and nuance,‚Äù he says. ‚ÄúWhat you write about is not a fossilized bit of commentary for a blog post. When you learn more, you add to it. It‚Äôs less about shock and rage; it‚Äôs more connective.‚Äù In an age of doom-scrolling and Zoom fatigue, some digital-garden enthusiasts say the internet they live in is, as Caulfield puts it, ‚Äúoptimistically hopeful.‚Äù

While many people are searching for more intimate communities on the internet, not everyone can spin up a digital garden: you need to be able to do at least some rudimentary coding. Making a page from scratch affords more creative freedom than social-media and web-hosting sites that let you drag and drop elements onto your page, but it can be daunting and time-consuming.

Chris Biscardi is trying to get rid of that barrier to entry with a text editor for digital gardens that‚Äôs still in its alpha stage. Called Toast, it‚Äôs ‚Äúsomething you might experience with Wordpress,‚Äù he says.

Ultimately, whether digital gardens will be an escapist remnant of 2020‚Äôs hellscape or wither in the face of easier social media remains to be seen. ‚ÄúI‚Äôm interested in seeing how it plays out,‚Äù Appleton says.

‚ÄúFor some people it‚Äôs a reaction to social media, and for others it‚Äôs a trend,‚Äù Critchlow says. ‚ÄúWhether or not it will hit critical mass ‚Ä¶ that‚Äôs to be seen.‚Äù

About

I was inspired by the MIT Technology Review article, Maggie Appleton's write-up about the history of digital gardens, and Annika Hansteen-Izora's story in Deem Journal to create my own Digital Garden.

I was also influenced by my earlier explorations around the Internet and websites (documented in this spreadsheet, Mindy Seu and Ursula K. Le Guin's ideas surrounding the act of "gathering," Kenneth Goldsmith's writings on folk archiving, and creative coding.

How to Create a Garden

I think creating a digital garden is such a great and fun way to keep track of the things you're interested in, such as research for your thesis, notes on coding or 3D modeling, your favorite recipes and cooking tricks, etc. You can also use an online digital garden to share your knowledge with others, but I ultimately think digital gardens should benefit you. You can start and stop at any time, but I think digital gardens are mostly enriching when you're able to tend to them often.

If you're a non-coder, I think the easiest way to create a digital garden is through Notion or Google Sheets. You can create shareable pages using these platforms. I think Notion can be a particularly sophisticated tool to create a personal "wiki" because you can create databases, toggle lists, link to other pages, embed YouTube videos or files, etc. You can even create calendars, kanban boards, etc. So, your digital garden could even evolve into a productivity tool if you really wanted it to. With that being said, I think it's not always helpful to think of digital gardens as an activity for "productivity." Digital gardens are meant to be an evolving receptacle for all of the knowledge you've gained over time and can always refer back to or share with others.

If you're a coder, your websites can have literally no CSS or you can go really crazy with the design if that's more up your alley. Regardless, it should bring you joy and satisfaction. If you just need a place to keep track of your notes, basic HTML will work just fine.
